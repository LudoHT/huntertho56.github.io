Case Study

Post-Admit Checklist Modernization

College of Coastal Georgia · Element451 · Admissions, Housing, Registrar, Financial Aid, Bursar, Advising, Health Center, Academic Support & Technology Services

01 · Overview

After a student gets admitted, there's a gap between "you're in" and the first day of classes — orientation, housing, financial aid, immunizations, advising — and at most colleges that gap is held together by static PDFs, spreadsheets, and a lot of manual follow-up. This project moved that post-admit experience into a dynamic, trackable checklist inside Element451, so admitted students could see exactly what they still needed to do, and staff across departments could see exactly where things stood without chasing it down themselves.

02 · The Problem

Post-admit requirements were scattered across departments and mostly static — a checklist that looked the same for every admitted student regardless of their actual situation. That meant items that didn't apply to a given student still showed up, items that did apply weren't always visible at the right time, and "complete" usually meant someone on staff had manually checked a record and updated something by hand. Multiplied across every admitted student and every department involved, that's a lot of manual tracking, and a lot of room for a student to miss something simply because the system never told them it applied to them.

03 · My Role

  • Helped build and configure post-admit checklist items in Element451.
  • Created checklist logic tied to application and decision data.
  • Used condition-based checklist items so requirements only appeared when relevant to a given student.
  • Supported custom fields that stored checklist completion status behind the scenes.
  • Helped connect external system data to Element451 through imports and SFTP processes.
  • Worked across Admissions, Technology Services, Housing, Registrar, Financial Aid, Bursar, Advising, the Health Center, and Academic Support to get requirements right for each department.
  • Helped reduce manual tracking by letting checklist items complete themselves through rules, student input, custom fields, and imported data — instead of someone updating a record by hand.

04 · What I Built / Helped Implement

The core of this was a checklist experience that actually adapts to the student looking at it. Checklist items live in Element451's Decisions module, and each one can be active or inactive, required or optional, scoped to post-admit, and condition-based — meaning it only shows up if the student's data says it should. Behind that, custom fields track completion status quietly in the background, supplemental forms let a student's own input trigger an item's visibility or mark it complete, and SFTP/import processes feed in data from other systems to update those same fields. Put together, a student logs in and sees a checklist that reflects their actual situation, and staff get a record that updates itself instead of one they have to maintain by hand.

05 · Technical Approach

A few things had to work together correctly for this to hold up:

  • Checklist items in the Decisions module configured as active/inactive, required/optional, post-admit, and condition-based, so the same checklist template could behave differently per student.
  • Custom fields used as the source of truth for completion status, separate from whatever triggered that status.
  • Supplemental forms wired up so a student's submission could both capture their input and flip the checklist item's visibility or completion state.
  • SFTP/import processes that update records using student identifiers and status fields — Element451 matches the incoming data to the correct student, updates the relevant custom field, and the checklist logic marks the item complete based on how it's configured.

None of these pieces are exotic on their own — the work was in getting the conditions, fields, and data sources to agree with each other consistently across a few thousand student records and several departments' worth of requirements.

06 · Results

This is still early — the dynamic post-admit checklist went live for Fall 2026 admitted-student processes, so what follows are early indicators from that first term, not an audited, long-term institutional result. With that caveat:

  • Early indicators show stronger admitted-student engagement with post-admit requirements overall.
  • Orientation registration increased by an estimated 18%.
  • Housing applications increased by an estimated 22%.
  • Completion of required post-admit information before the first day of classes improved by an estimated 26–30%.

As the process runs across more terms, I'd expect completion rates, student clarity, and staff visibility to keep improving — this is the first data point, not the ceiling.

07 · What I Learned

Most of the hard part wasn't Element451 configuration — it was getting Admissions, Housing, Registrar, Financial Aid, Bursar, Advising, the Health Center, and Academic Support to agree on what "required" and "complete" actually mean for their piece of the checklist, and translating that into conditions and fields that don't contradict each other. That's a cross-department coordination problem before it's a technical one, and it's part of why I've gotten more interested in the human side of these systems — not just whether something is technically configured correctly, but whether the person looking at it on the other end actually understands what it's asking them to do.

Case Study

Service Workflow Automation System

Freelance · AWS Serverless · Amazon Connect · Built for service-based small businesses (plumbing & HVAC)

01 · Overview

This is a reusable workflow automation system I built for service-based small businesses — plumbing and HVAC-style companies, specifically — that replaces manual phone-and-paper intake with a structured, automated request pipeline. It's not a chatbot bolted onto a website. It's a full intake-to-ticket workflow built on AWS serverless architecture, with an optional AI-assisted intake path layered on top for after-hours calls or when nobody's at the desk to answer.

02 · The Problem

Small service businesses run on phone calls and whoever happens to be at the desk to take them. When that person isn't available — after hours, mid-job, out for the day — requests either don't get captured cleanly or get captured with half the details missing, which means someone has to call the customer back just to fill in the gaps. That doesn't scale, and it doesn't leave a clean, technician-ready record of what was actually requested.

03 · My Role

  • Designed and built the serverless backend using AWS S3, Lambda, and API Gateway.
  • Designed the structured intake schema — name, contact info, address, service type, issue description, urgency, and preferred follow-up method.
  • Built the email routing so a completed, technician-ready request lands directly with the business.
  • Designed the AI-assisted intake workflow concept using Amazon Connect for after-hours and hands-off scenarios.
  • Worked directly with a real business — Karate's Plumbing — to validate the workflow against how their office actually operates day to day.

04 · What I Built

At its core, the system gives customers or office staff a way to submit a service request online instead of relying entirely on a phone call — capturing the same structured information every time: name, contact info, address, service type, issue description, urgency, and how the customer wants to be followed up with. That submission runs through a backend AWS workflow and comes out the other side as a completed request emailed straight to the business, formatted so a technician can pick it up and go without having to track anyone down for missing details. It's built to be adapted across multiple service-based businesses with similar intake needs — the same backend pattern, with the fields and routing tuned per business.

05 · Technical Approach

  • AWS S3 hosts the intake interface and static assets.
  • API Gateway is the entry point for every submission, regardless of how it came in.
  • AWS Lambda handles backend processing — validating and structuring the submitted data.
  • Email routing fires automatically once a request is processed, with no manual step in between.
  • The intake schema and routing logic are kept separate from the backend itself, so the same architecture can be reused for a different service business without a rebuild.

06 · AI-Assisted Intake

The system also includes an AI-assisted intake path built on Amazon Connect — not as a separate product, but as another door into the same backend. A customer calls in, answers a defined set of questions, and those responses get turned into the same structured service-request format as a web submission, then sent through the same pipeline to reach the business. The point isn't "add a chatbot" — it's giving the system a way to capture a clean request even when nobody's available to answer the phone.

07 · Business Impact

These are estimates based on early business feedback and observed workflow improvement, not audited metrics:

  • Reduced manual intake and ticket-creation effort by an estimated 30–40%.
  • Reduced customer callback needs by an estimated 25–35%.
  • Saved roughly 3–5 administrative hours per week for a small service office.
  • Improved after-hours request capture by an estimated 20–30%.
  • Reduced incomplete service-request details by an estimated 25%.

For Karate's Plumbing specifically, the practical feedback was simpler than any of those numbers: the office secretary didn't have to be physically present to handle intake anymore. She could answer calls remotely and submit the ticket herself, plumbers picked up requests with clearer information already attached, and the business lost less time calling customers back just to collect details that should've been captured the first time — especially on after-hours requests.

08 · What I Learned

The useful insight here was separating "how a request comes in" from "what a request needs to contain." Once those are decoupled, a phone call, a web form, and an AI-assisted intake call can all feed the exact same backend without the business ever seeing a difference in what lands in their inbox. It also reinforced something I keep running into at a much larger scale in my day job: the simplest automation that a small office will actually use beats a more sophisticated one that doesn't fit how they actually work.
